HTML Quotation and Citation Elements

In this section we'll look at < blockquote >, < q >, < abbr >, < address >, < cite >, and < bdo > HTML elements.

Example of a blockquote:

Here is a quote from the PURRFECTPOST website:

Cats are hilarious. Really. Just ask the internet. They do the cutest, sweetest things you can imagine, and they can really lower their humans' stress levels. Nothing beats coming home after a challenging day and watching your cat romp around after his toys, acting like a clown.

HTML < q > for Short Quotations:

The HTML < q > tag defines a short quotation. Browsers normally instert quotation marks around the quotation.

The Oregon Humane Society rescues, heals and adopts more than 11,000 pets each year.

HTML < abbr > for Abbreviations

The cfa has been celebrating cats since 1906.

HTML < address > for Contact Information

The HTML < address > tag defines the contact information for the author/owner of a document or an article.

The contact information can be an email address, URL, physical address, phone number, social media handle, etc.

The text in the address element usually renders in italic, and browsers will always add a line break before and after the address element.

Example

Written by Liz Eymann.
Visit us at:
example.com
Box 123, Catland
USA

HTML < cite > for Work Title

The HTML cite tag defines the title of a creative work (e.g. a book, a poem, a song, a movie, a painting, a scultpure, etc.)

Note: A person's name is not the title of a work.

The text in the < cite > element usually renders in italic

Example

Madonna of the Cat by Federico Barocci. Engravings of the painting appeared in 1577.

HTML < bdo > for Bi-Directional Override
